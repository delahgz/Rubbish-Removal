End of Lease Rubbish Removal Checklist

Moving out of a rental? The condition you leave the property in directly affects whether you get your bond back. Leaving rubbish, unwanted furniture, or items in storage areas is one of the most common reasons landlords make bond deductions.

This checklist covers everything you need to remove before handing back the keys, plus what happens if you leave items behind.

What needs to be removed

Your lease agreement typically requires you to return the property in the same condition as when you moved in (allowing for fair wear and tear). In practice, this means removing everything you brought in, plus anything that has accumulated.

End of lease removal checklist

  • All furniture you brought (including beds, mattresses, sofas)
  • Appliances not provided by the landlord
  • Contents of all cupboards and wardrobes
  • Garage and storage cage contents
  • Garden waste (if you created it)
  • Rubbish in bins (empty all bins)
  • Items in shared storage areas
  • Curtains or blinds you installed
  • Wall hooks, picture hangers, and any wall damage
  • Outdoor furniture and pot plants
Important: Check your lease and ingoing condition report. If a item was there when you moved in (like a washing machine or outdoor setting), you cannot remove it. If something is missing or broken, the landlord can claim from your bond.

Common items people forget

Storage cages and garages

Out of sight, out of mind. Storage areas accumulate boxes, old bikes, and forgotten furniture over the course of a lease. Many tenants forget to check these areas until the final inspection.

Built-in wardrobes

Empty the shelves, but also check the floor and top shelf. Items get pushed to the back and forgotten.

Garden sheds

Garden tools, pot plants, old paints, and miscellaneous items often accumulate in sheds. If you put it there, you need to remove it.

Under the house or in roof spaces

If you stored anything in crawl spaces or roof access areas, it needs to come out.

What happens if you leave items behind

In NSW, landlords must give you a chance to collect abandoned goods, but they can charge you for storage and disposal costs. These costs come directly from your bond.

Typical charges landlords or agents pass on include:

  • Professional cleaning (if items prevent proper cleaning)
  • Skip bin hire for larger amounts of waste
  • Rubbish removal service fees
  • Agent time for coordinating removal
  • Storage fees if goods need to be held

These costs typically far exceed what it would cost you to arrange removal yourself before you hand back the keys.

Pro tip: Book your rubbish removal for the day before your final inspection, not the day of. This gives you time to clean properly once the items are gone.

Council cleanup is not the answer

Sydney councils offer free hard rubbish collection, but there are problems relying on this for end-of-lease:

  • Bookings can take 2-4 weeks, which may be after your lease ends
  • Limited to 2-3 cubic metres per collection
  • Mattresses and some items are not accepted
  • Items must be placed on the kerb, which is not always possible in apartments

If your move-out date is fixed (and it usually is), you need a solution that works on your timeline.

How to make end of lease removal easier

Start early

Begin sorting and removing items two weeks before your move-out date. This reduces stress and gives you options if something goes wrong.

Take photos

Document the property condition after you remove your items but before the final clean. This protects you in bond disputes.

Communicate with your agent

If you are leaving anything (like a barbecue the next tenant might want), ask first. Never assume the landlord will be fine with it.

Book removal and cleaning in order

Rubbish removal first, then professional cleaning. Cleaners cannot clean around furniture and boxes.
